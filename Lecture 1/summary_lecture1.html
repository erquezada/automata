Why Study Automata? - Summary

In this introductory lecture, the professor discusses the fundamental motivations behind studying automata theory in computer science. The key points covered are as follows:

1. **Problem Solvability:** In most computer science classes, students are presented with problems and tasked with designing programs to solve them. The assumption is that these problems have solutions, and the focus is on crafting an appropriate program. The specifics of how the program is implemented in the computer are often considered irrelevant.

2. **Real-World Challenges:** In real-life scenarios, problems arise from practical needs, not from instructors. However, not all real-world problems are solvable by algorithms. Some problems may be too general or ask for optimal solutions that require unrealistic computation time.

3. **Objectives of the Automata Class:** The primary objectives of the Automata class are to teach students that not all problems are solvable and why, and to help them understand which general problems are solvable. It's important to grasp when a problem is unsolvable and to formulate more specific problems with solutions. Students will explore the basics in this one-semester class, but a deeper understanding can be gained through graduate classes on the Theory of Computation.

4. **Understanding Computation:** To determine which problems are algorithmically solvable, it's necessary to understand precisely what a computer does when executing a program written in a programming language. This complex process forms a significant part of the course.

5. **The Compilation Process:** Many people perceive computers as "magic boxes" that understand program instructions. However, this understanding is not magic but based on straightforward algorithms. The course aims to shed light on how programs are compiled into specific sequences of actions.

6. **Caution and Real-World Applications:** The class will focus on the basics of compiling, while real-world compilers employ additional advanced concepts. Students interested in practical compiling can consider taking a Compiler class when available.

7. **Challenges and Success Strategies:** This class will be challenging for students as it involves not only algorithm design but also proofs of unsolvability. Strategies for success include submitting homework on time, asking questions when in doubt, forming study groups, and active problem-solving through exercises.

8. **Studying for Success:** Active learning through exercises is emphasized as the most effective way to grasp the material. Students are encouraged to seek help when needed and to focus on understanding each step in proofs. Accuracy and precision in using definitions and proofs are vital.

9. **Assurance of Success:** The professor reassures students that most will pass the class, given their demonstrated ability to handle complex subjects in the past. The only path to failure is to neglect homework and studying.

In conclusion, the Automata class introduces students to the concept that not all problems are solvable by algorithms and aims to provide a deep understanding of computation and the compilation process. Success in the class is attainable through active engagement, problem-solving, and seeking assistance when needed. The professor expresses confidence in the students' ability to succeed in the course.
